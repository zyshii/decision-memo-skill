# Translation Guide — Chinese to English Part B

The English version of Part B is not a word-for-word translation. It's a re-authoring for English-speaking interviewers. Apply these principles.

## Core Principle

Chinese and English BQ narratives have different default registers. Chinese professional speech tends toward structured modesty with explicit framing. English professional speech tends toward concise self-authorship with implicit framing. Translation should adjust register, not just vocabulary.

---

## Register Adjustments

### Confidence calibration

Chinese: "我认为这个方案更符合用户心智"
Literal English: "I believe this option is more aligned with user mental models"
Adjusted English: "Option B mapped better to how users actually approach the task"

Reason: English interview speech sounds more credible when observations are stated as conclusions rather than beliefs. "I believe" weakens unless the speaker intends to signal uncertainty.

### Seniority signaling

Chinese: "这对我从 mid-level 向 senior-level 转变很有启发"
Literal English: "This was enlightening for my mid-to-senior transition"
Adjusted English: "That shift has been one of the more meaningful steps in my move from mid-level execution to senior-level influence"

Reason: English register allows one direct self-positioning statement per story. More than one sounds performative. Pick the strongest placement — usually the Result.

### Hedging density

Chinese BQ often uses multiple hedges per sentence ("可能"、"也许"、"一定程度上"). English should concentrate hedging into single modal verbs or cut it entirely when the user is stating something they stand behind.

---

## Structural Adjustments

### STAR length balance

Chinese STAR tends to front-load Situation and Task. English STAR should weight Action more heavily — roughly 50-60% of total length. Action is where senior-level judgment is visible.

If the Chinese Situation is three sentences, the English Situation should be one or two.

### Transition phrases

Chinese uses explicit connectors ("所以"、"因此"、"更重要的是"). English often drops these and lets the logic show through sentence order.

Chinese: "我识别出一个 pattern。所以我从说服策略转向治理策略。"
Adjusted English: "I spotted a pattern I'd been missing — the repeated pushbacks weren't a communication problem, they were a governance problem."

Reason: English favors one compressed sentence with an em-dash over two short sentences with a connector. Sounds more senior.

---

## Vocabulary Choices

### Domain terminology

Use the precise English term from the domain, not the direct translation:

| Chinese | Weak English | Better English |
|---|---|---|
| 治理 | management | governance |
| 话语权 | speaking power | authority / voice |
| 落地 | land on the ground | land / ship / hold |
| 踩坑 | step in a pit | run into a pitfall / hit a wall |
| 对齐 | align | align (keep) / sync / close the gap |
| 拍板 | strike the board | call the shot / make the final call |
| 赛马 | horse race | run parallel options |
| 战略定力 | strategic stability | strategic conviction |

### Modesty markers that don't translate

Phrases like "我只是" / "我尝试" / "我们一起" often carry Chinese-language politeness that translates to English as self-erasure. Evaluate each:

- Kept: when the user genuinely wants to credit others or flag uncertainty
- Cut: when it's reflex politeness that would underposition the user

---

## Voice Preservation

Despite these adjustments, the English version should still sound like the user. Apply the adjustments as a floor, not a ceiling — don't over-polish until the voice disappears.

Good signal: the user reads the English version aloud and it flows naturally in their speech.
Bad signal: the user says "this sounds like a cover letter" or "I wouldn't actually talk like this."

When in doubt, ask: "Read this out loud — does it sound like you?"

---

## Hook Question Translation

Hook questions and their answers translate with two additional considerations:

### Question phrasing

English hook questions should sound like real interviewer language, not translated:

- Chinese original: "你觉得这次你失败了吗？"
- Literal: "Do you feel you failed this time?"
- Adjusted: "Do you think you failed here?"

Use "here" not "this time." Use "think" not "feel" unless emotion is explicitly the topic.

### Answer opening

English answers should usually start with a directional verb or position statement, not a hedged acknowledgment:

- Weak opening: "Well, I think it's important to consider..."
- Strong opening: "I didn't win the design argument, but..."

The first six words of a hook answer either land the position or lose the room.

## Common Traps

### Trap 1: Translating titles and roles literally

- "大老板" → "big boss" (wrong) → "leadership" / "the VP" / "my skip-level" (right)
- "前辈" → "senior" (wrong, ambiguous with job level) → "the senior designer who'd been there longer" (right when context matters) or drop

### Trap 2: Preserving Chinese sentence structure

Chinese sentences often end with the verb or the key assertion. English expects the key assertion early.

- Chinese: "在这样的组织里，通过治理而非说服建立影响力，我觉得是一个关键的认知。"
- Literal: "In this kind of organization, through governance rather than persuasion to build influence, I think is a key realization."
- Adjusted: "In orgs with asymmetric authority, influence comes from governance, not persuasion. That was the key realization."

### Trap 3: Over-formalizing

Professional English in interview contexts is more conversational than written Chinese professional prose. Contractions ("I'd," "couldn't," "that's") are appropriate and make speech sound more natural.
